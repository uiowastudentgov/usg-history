Where We Came From

USG's Origins

Student governance at the University of Iowa is older than most people realize, and its origins are murkier than you might expect. This is the story of how an unnamed student body became one of the most structured undergraduate governments in the Big Ten.

Lost to History: The Pre-1919 Era

Student governance at Iowa traces back to the late 1800s, but no official records document when it began or what it was called. The exact founding date is genuinely lost to history. What we know is that by the early 20th century, students were already organizing formally, debating, voting, and passing decisions that shaped campus life. The earliest surviving document is a handwritten meeting ledger from Fall 1919, now held in the University Archives.

A Century of Name Changes

USG has never had just one name. It began as a loose Student Council in the late 1800s, formally organized under that name through the mid-20th century, and was renamed to Student Senate on May 10, 1961. In the 1980s it became UISA (University of Iowa Student Association), before adopting its current identity as USG, University of Iowa Student Government, in Fall 1994. Each rename reflected a shift in how students understood their role on campus.

The 1916-1919 Awakening

The earliest documented controversy in USG's history is the Junior Prom Committee Incident of March 16, 1916, a dispute over campus event control that foreshadowed decades of student-administration tension. By Fall 1919, a structured Student Council had formally emerged. On December 5, 1919, students passed the first ever recorded resolution, establishing the legislative tradition that defines USG to this day.

When Three Branches Became Law

For most of its early history, USG operated as a single legislative body. Everything changed on March 12, 1958, when the organization formally adopted a three-branch structure, Legislative, Executive, and Judicial, mirroring the U.S. federal government. That same year, the first Chief Justice of the Student Judicial Court was elected. This architecture has governed USG ever since, and is one of the most sophisticated structures of any student government in the country.

🏛️

Legislative Branch

The Senate, composed of elected senators from colleges across campus, introduces, debates, and passes resolutions, bills, and funding allocations. The backbone of student representation since 1919.

⚡

Executive Branch

Led by the President and Vice President (elected by the full student body), the Executive implements Senate decisions, represents students to administration, and coordinates inter-branch affairs. Formalized in 1958.

⚖️

Judicial Branch

The Student Judicial Court, headed by the Chief Justice, interprets USG's constitution, adjudicates elections disputes, and ensures the other branches stay within their authority. First Chief Justice elected Fall 1958.

Our Foundation

The 1942 Mission Statement

In November 1942, USG's predecessor formally adopted a written mission statement, one of the most important early milestones in the organization's history. Over 80 years later, its core ideas remain embedded in everything USG does.

Adopted November 1942, Student Council

  • To encourage complete student interest and participation in campus affairs.
  • To coordinate and promote the activities of student groups.
  • To reflect student opinion and serve as a group with which the faculty may consult for a student consensus in various situations as they arise.

This mission statement was drawn from an original typed document dated November 13, 1942, preserved in USG's historical archive.

History

The Full Timeline

From its scrappy origins in the late 1800s to a fully modernized student government in the 2020s, this timeline captures USG's most important moments, milestones, and memorable oddities.

This is a living document. New entries should be added each academic year by the incoming Executive team in consultation with the Historian. Page numbers in parentheses refer to Taegan's Official USG History Document.
Era I
The Founding Era · Late 1800s, 1944

Student governance begins with no official records, early resolutions, and the slow emergence of a formal council structure.

Feb 25, 1847 Founding
University of Iowa Founded
The University of Iowa is officially established. Student governance would not follow for several decades, but the institution that USG serves is born on this date.
Late 1800s Origin
Student Government Created (No Official Date Recorded)
At some point in the late 19th century, University of Iowa students organized a formal governance body. The exact date is lost to history, no official records survive from this period.
The earliest form of USG predates the automobile, the Wright Brothers' first flight, and even Kinnick Stadium itself.
March 16, 1916 Drama
Junior Prom Committee Incident
An early recorded controversy involving the Junior Prom Committee, one of the first documented instances of student governance grappling with campus event politics. (pp. 6-7)
Fall 1919 First
Earliest Known Record of USG, The First Council Emerges
The first documented record of the University of Iowa Student Government organization dates to Fall 1919. This is the baseline of the entire historical archive. (pp. 7-8)
The surviving 1919 meeting minutes are handwritten in a bound ledger, still preserved in the University Archives today.
December 5, 1919 First
First Recorded Resolution in USG History
The very first formal resolution ever recorded by the student government is passed on this date, setting the legislative tradition that continues over 100 years later. (p. 9)
1921-1922 Structure
Representation Crisis
An early and significant internal conflict over how students should be represented within the Council, foreshadowing ongoing debates about equity and proportionality in student governance. (pp. 10-12)
1923 Campus Life
Green Cap Policy
The Student Council passes the Green Cap Policy, requiring first-year male students to wear green caps as a form of freshman identification. A very different time! (p. 12)
The Green Cap tradition was common at many Midwestern universities in the 1920s. Iowa freshmen were required to wear these caps until meeting certain academic or social criteria.
November 13, 1942 Milestone
First Official Mission Statement Adopted
The Student Council formally adopts a written mission statement for the first time, a three-point declaration of purpose that forms the philosophical backbone of the organization to this day.
1944-1945 Policy
Regulations Governing Senior Privileges for Women Adopted
A set of paternalistic regulations were adopted restricting the autonomy of senior women on campus, including provisions requiring parental notification and consent for access to "senior privileges." A reminder of how much the organization has changed on gender equity. (p. 15)
These regulations required that if a senior woman's parents objected to her receiving senior privileges, she would be "bound by standard regulations governing undergraduate women." Context matters.
Era II
Post-War Growth · 1945, 1960

USG gains its first physical office, fights for civil rights, and begins shaping the campus landscape we know today.

January 9, 1945 Structure
12-Member Student Council Formation
A formal 12-member Student Council structure is established, creating a more defined legislative body and setting the stage for future structural reforms. (p. 15)
1945 Campus
Straw Poll for Nile Kinnick Stadium Naming
Students participate in a straw poll related to the naming of what would become Nile Kinnick Stadium, honoring the beloved Hawkeye who gave his life in World War II. (pp. 15-16)
Nile Kinnick won the Heisman Trophy in 1939 and was killed in action in 1943. He is one of the most celebrated figures in Iowa sports history.
May 7, 1946 Civil Rights
Non-Discrimination Resolution, Creation of Diversity & Inclusion Framework
In one of the most significant early acts of the Student Council, a resolution is passed affirming that all students should have access to university facilities regardless of race, creed, or color. A standing inter-racial and inter-religious committee is established to study campus equity issues. (p. 16)
This resolution passed in 1946, eight years before the Supreme Court's Brown v. Board of Education decision. USG was early.
April 1946 Remarkable
Herb Olson Elected After German Prison Camp
Herb Olson is elected Student Council President after returning from a German prisoner-of-war camp. His election is a powerful postwar symbol of student resilience. (pp. 16-17)
Olson ran for Student Council President after surviving captivity as a WWII POW. Few USG presidents have had a more dramatic road to office.
Spring 1948 First
Iowa Legislature Begins Funding Student Council, $1,000 Annually
A milestone in external recognition: the Iowa State Legislature appropriates $1,000 per year to fund the Student Council's operations, legitimizing it as an important civic institution. (p. 19)
March 1950 First
USG Secures Its First Physical Office on Campus
After years of operating without a dedicated space, the Student Council finally obtains its first campus office, a moment that transformed the organization from a loose body into a real institution. (p. 22)
The PowerPoint notes that USG "worked until 1955" to get this office, meaning it took six full years of lobbying before the administration made space for student government.
May 1950 Civil Rights
Votes to End Race & Religion Questions on University Applications
The Student Council passes a resolution urging the University to remove questions about race and religion from admissions applications, an early and meaningful equity victory. (p. 21)
Fall 1950 First
First Parking-Related Legislation
The first-ever piece of parking legislation is introduced, a tradition that continues to animate USG debates to this day. (p. 21)
Over 70 years later, parking on campus remains one of the most consistent student grievances. Some things never change.
October 22, 1952 Win
USG Negotiates 1,100+ Additional Seats for Students at Kinnick
The Student Council successfully advocates for over 1,100 more student seats at Kinnick Stadium. USG also gains formal seating negotiation rights, a significant expansion of its institutional power. (p. 24)
June 5, 1954 Policy
First Recorded Opposition to Rising Tuition Costs
USG passes its first formal objection to tuition increases, initiating a tradition of tuition advocacy that spans every subsequent decade of the organization's history. (p. 26)
In 1954, annual tuition at UI was approximately $100. Today it exceeds $9,000 for in-state students. USG has been fighting this fight for 70+ years.
Spring 1955 Institutional
Student Council Moves Office to the Iowa Memorial Union
USG relocates its offices to the Iowa Memorial Union, the home it has occupied in various forms ever since. The IMU becomes central to USG's identity and accessibility. (p. 27)
April 14, 1956 Workers' Rights
First Successful Lobby to Increase Student Wages
USG successfully lobbies for an increase in student employee wages, one of the earliest recorded examples of the organization winning a direct economic benefit for students. Minimum wage of $1/hour for student employment is formally established. (p. 29)
March 12, 1958 Landmark
Three-Branch Government Structure Officially Implemented
USG formally adopts its modern tri-branch structure, Legislative, Executive, and Judicial, mirroring the U.S. federal government. This restructuring defines the organization's architecture to this day. (p. 32)
USG's three-branch system predates the formal establishment of checks-and-balances in many peer student governments across the Big Ten.
Fall 1958 First
First Chief Justice of the Student Judicial Court Elected
Following the 1958 structural reforms, the first Chief Justice of USG's Student Judicial Court is elected, establishing the judicial branch as a functioning institution. (p. 33)
April 8, 1959 Historic
First Female Student Council President Elected
A landmark moment in USG history: the first woman is elected to serve as President of the Student Council, breaking a barrier in a year when gender barriers were still common across American institutions. (p. 35)
This happened five years before the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Iowa's students were ahead of the curve.
Era III
The Protest Era · 1961, 1979

Renamed, expanded, and energized, USG navigates Vietnam, civil unrest, and transformative campus change.

May 10, 1961 Rename
Student Council Renamed to Student Senate
After decades as the "Student Council," the organization officially adopts the name "Student Senate", reflecting its more formal legislative identity. The Senate structure would persist until the 1994 USG rebrand. (p. 43)
Spring 1961 First
First Meeting Between Student Council and State-Level Political Figures
USG meets formally with state-level politicians for the first time, inaugurating a tradition of political engagement beyond campus that would grow significantly in subsequent decades. (p. 43)
1962 Policy
Resolution Requiring Non-Discrimination Agreements from Landlords
The Senate approves a resolution requiring landlords renting to UI students to sign non-discrimination agreements, an early form of off-campus tenant protection. (p. 49)
1963 Win
ROTC Made Voluntary for Male Students
The Student Senate successfully petitions President Hancher to make ROTC participation voluntary, a significant autonomy win during the lead-up to the Vietnam era. (p. 51)
1964 CAMBUS
Student Senate Passes Resolution Advocating for CAMBUS
The Senate passes a resolution advocating for a downtown bus system, planting the seed for CAMBUS, which would launch in 1972 and become one of the most-used campus transit systems in the country. (p. 53)
CAMBUS now provides millions of rides per year. It all started with a USG resolution in 1964.
1965 Campus Life
Student Senate Considers Bill to Ban Undergrad Driving on Campus
An early and ambitious transportation policy is discussed, banning all undergraduate student driving on campus. The bill is considered but not passed. (p. 55)
The parking problem was so bad in 1965 that banning cars entirely seemed like a reasonable policy discussion. Sound familiar?
Fall 1966 Structure
SABAC Established, Senate Auditing and Budgeting Committee
The Student Senate creates SABAC (Senate Auditing and Budgeting Committee), formalizing financial oversight of student organization funding. This structure remains foundational to USG's fiscal role today. (p. 58)
Fall 1967 Vietnam Era
Senate Condemns University Inaction on Vietnam Protests
During one of the most turbulent periods in American campus history, the Student Senate passes a resolution formally condemning the University's lack of response to major Vietnam War protests on campus. A subsequent resolution also condemns disciplinary action taken against students arrested at protests. (p. 64)
Multiple Daily Iowan front pages from this era show student-led boycotts and protests, some of the most politically charged moments in USG's history.
Summer 1968 Structure
GPSG (Graduate Professional Student Government) Created
The Student Senate passes a resolution creating a separate graduate student government, recognizing that graduate and professional students have distinct needs. GPSG and USG would later split entirely in 2007. (p. 68)
1969 First
Public Course & Professor Evaluations Released, A First for the University
The Student Senate begins publishing public evaluations of courses and professors, a first for the University of Iowa and an early form of academic accountability. (p. 70)
USG pioneered professor evaluations decades before RateMyProfessors.com existed.
1972 CAMBUS
CAMBUS Services Launch
Eight years after USG first advocated for a campus bus system, CAMBUS officially launches, becoming one of USG's most lasting infrastructure legacies. Students ride for free.
1979 Civil Rights
UI Rejects Calls to Expel the UI Iranian Student Association
Following the Iranian hostage crisis, calls emerge to expel Iranian student organizations from campus. USG and the University successfully resist these demands, a principled stand for inclusion during a politically charged moment. (p. 70+)
Era IV
The Modern Era · 1980s, 2000s

USG finds its modern name, expands services, and enters the digital age.

1989 First
President Pepe, First Big Ten Student Government Conference
Under President Pepe, USG participates in the inaugural Big Ten Student Government Conference, formalizing inter-university collaboration between Big Ten student governments for the first time.
1996 Campus Life
USG Distributes Flyers on Fake ID Identification
USG takes a harm-reduction approach to underage drinking by distributing informational flyers showing what fake IDs look like, a creative intervention aimed at student safety. The same organization would later oppose a 21-only bar rule in 2004.
USG opposed both underage drinking AND the 21-only bar ordinance. The distinction: they wanted students safe, but also wanted 18-20 year olds to have community spaces.
1997 Campus
Pressure Administration to Renovate Classrooms
USG formally lobbies administration to invest in classroom renovation across campus, one of many examples of the organization using political pressure to improve daily academic life.
1998 SafeWalk
SafeWalk Evening Escort Service Launched; IMU Kept Open Until 2AM
USG introduces SafeWalk, an evening escort service for students navigating campus at night. The organization also successfully keeps the Iowa Memorial Union open until 2AM for studying students.
SafeWalk emerged from student safety concerns and remains one of the most student-visible services USG has championed.
1999 Digital
Online Voting for USG Introduced; $16,700 to RVAP
USG adopts online voting for student elections, a significant accessibility improvement. The organization also provides $16,700 in emergency funding to RVAP (Rape Victim Advocacy Program) during a period of financial struggle.
Fall 1994 Rename
UISA Rebrands to USG, The Name We Know Today
The University of Iowa Student Association (UISA) officially rebrands to the University of Iowa Student Government (USG), the name the organization carries to this day. (p. 73)
The acronym "USG" has now been in use for over 30 years. Future sessions: keep it!
2002 Sustainability
First Recycling Project Comes to Campus
USG leads the charge on Iowa's first campus-wide recycling program, a sustainability milestone that predates most universities' environmental initiatives.
2004 Access
UIowa Collegiate Readership Program Launches
USG helps establish the Collegiate Readership Program, providing students with free access to major newspapers including USA Today and The New York Times across campus distribution points.
2005 Structure
City Council Liaison Position Created
USG establishes a City Council Liaison position, formally bridging the gap between student government and Iowa City municipal governance. A major expansion of USG's civic reach.
2007 Structure
USG and GPSG Formally Split
After decades of co-existence, USG and the Graduate Professional Student Government officially separate, allowing each body to focus exclusively on its student population. USG becomes a strictly undergraduate body. (p. 74)
2007 Transportation
Nite Ride Comes to UIowa
USG brings Nite Ride to campus, a safe late-night transportation service for students, addressing a long-standing gap in campus safety infrastructure.
Era V
The Digital & Reform Era · 2008-Present

USG goes transparent, expands outreach, and champions mental health, inclusion, and student safety in the 21st century.

2009 Transparency
USG Begins Live-Streaming Senate Proceedings
USG becomes an early adopter of video live-streaming for government proceedings, making Senate meetings accessible to any student with an internet connection. A major transparency win. (p. 81)
2009 Outreach
First Ever IMU Open House for New Students
USG hosts its inaugural Iowa Memorial Union Open House, introducing incoming students to campus resources and student government during Welcome Week. (p. 81)
Fall 2010 Accountability
Student Elections Commissioner Position Created
USG creates the Student Elections Commissioner position specifically to investigate election complaints and ensure fair electoral processes, an important accountability mechanism. (p. 82)
Fall 2013 🥞
"The Pancake Man" Event Created
USG launches "The Pancake Man", a beloved finals week tradition serving free pancakes to stressed students in the Iowa Memorial Union. One of USG's most popular student engagement events ever created. (p. 88)
Free pancakes during finals. Objectively the best thing USG has ever done. This tradition should never end.
Fall 2013 Shared Governance
Shared Governance Joint-Meetings Established
USG creates a formal joint-meeting structure with university administration and faculty, establishing a recurring, institutionalized forum for shared governance conversations. (p. 88)
2016 Major Year
2016: A Landmark Year for USG Policy
An extraordinary legislative year, USG passes or champions: Hawk Shop Sales Tax Elimination, Gender Neutral Bathrooms, International Student Tours, "All In" LLC partnership, Medical Amnesty Policy, "It's On Us" welcome week programming, and the Mental Health Fee.
The Medical Amnesty policy allows students to seek help in medical emergencies without fear of judicial punishment, a literal life-saving policy championed by USG.
2019 Tech
USG Senators Successfully Lobby for TopHat University Adoption
USG senators lobby successfully for the University's adoption of TopHat, an interactive classroom engagement platform that benefits thousands of students in large lecture courses. (p. 93)
2017-2018 This Document
11th Legislative Session History Retreat, This Document's Origin
The presentation from which much of this historical record is derived was created for the 11th Legislative Session Retreat, a tradition of passing institutional memory to new senators, executives, and justices joining USG each year.

Did You Know?

Fun Facts & Hidden History

Over a century of student governance means a century of unexpected, surprising, and genuinely entertaining moments. Here are some highlights you might not have learned in orientation.

🚗

Parking Problems Are Ancient

The first parking legislation at USG was introduced in Fall 1950. Students have been raising this issue for over 74 years.

🪖

A POW Became President

In April 1946, Herb Olson was elected Student Council President after returning from a German prisoner-of-war camp. Possibly the most remarkable origin story of any USG president in history.

🎓

First Female President: 1959

Iowa elected its first female Student Council President in 1959, five years before the Civil Rights Act and the same year Alaska and Hawaii joined the Union.

🚌

USG Made CAMBUS

CAMBUS, one of the busiest university transit systems in America, traces its roots directly to a 1964 Student Senate resolution. Eight years of advocacy turned into free bus rides for all students.

📋

We Rated Professors First

USG began publishing public course and professor evaluations in 1969, nearly 30 years before RateMyProfessors.com launched in 1999.

🥞

The Pancake Man Legacy

Founded in 2013, The Pancake Man offers free pancakes during finals week at the IMU and has become one of the most consistently attended events USG hosts each year.

📰

USG's Earliest Record: Handwritten

The oldest known USG document is a handwritten ledger from 1919, preserved in the University Archives.

⚖️

Three Branches Since 1958

USG adopted its three-branch governmental structure (Legislative, Executive, Judicial) in 1958, decades before most peer student governments at Big Ten institutions had comparable structures.

💡

The 1955 Seating Demand

In 1955, one of USG's early platform items was demanding more seating at football games AND more lighting in the west library parking lot. Priorities: sports and safety, in that order.

Why It Matters

Why USG's History Is Important

Institutional memory is a form of power. When organizations forget where they came from, they repeat old mistakes, reinvent existing solutions, and lose sight of their purpose. USG has been fighting for students for over a century. Here is why that history should matter to everyone who walks through the door.

📜 History Prevents Reinvention

  • The first parking legislation was introduced in 1950. If you're working on a parking resolution today, you're not starting from scratch, you're continuing a 74-year conversation. Know what's been tried.
  • CAMBUS took eight years of sustained advocacy (1964-1972) to become real. Major policy wins don't happen in one session. Knowing that history makes the long game easier to play.
  • The three-branch structure was a deliberate 1958 design choice. Understanding why it was built that way helps you work within it, and change it thoughtfully if needed.
  • USG has already solved many of the problems new members think are new. Check the archive before you draft.

🔥 History Shows What's Possible

  • In 1946, eight years before Brown v. Board, USG passed a non-discrimination resolution protecting students regardless of race, creed, or color. Students moved faster than the courts.
  • USG created CAMBUS, SafeWalk, Nite Ride, campus recycling, the Medical Amnesty policy, and gender-neutral bathrooms. These aren't abstract political wins, they are physical, daily realities on campus.
  • In 1959, USG elected its first female president. In an era when women had far fewer formal rights, Iowa's students chose differently. Precedent matters.
  • If USG feels like it cannot change anything, read the timeline.

⚠️ History Warns Us Too

  • In 1944-45, USG adopted regulations requiring parental notification and consent for senior women's privileges. Well-meaning governance can still produce paternalistic, harmful policy. Read the room, and read the record.
  • The 1921-22 Representation Crisis shows that structural fairness questions are never fully resolved. Every generation has to re-examine who has a seat and who doesn't.
  • The near-loss of USG's pre-1919 records is a cautionary tale. Institutions that don't document their history don't have one. That's why this document exists, and why you must keep updating it.
  • Absences, inaction, and apathy have consequences. The 1966 two-unexcused-absence policy wasn't arbitrary, it was a response to real disengagement. Show up.

🧭 History Is a Compass, Not a Chain

  • Knowing where USG has been doesn't mean you're obligated to repeat it. It means you can make informed decisions about when to honor tradition and when to break with it.
  • Every name change, Student Council, Student Senate, UISA, and finally USG, reflected a student body reimagining its own identity. You're allowed to do the same.
  • The students who wrote that first 1919 ledger in longhand had no idea what they were starting. The students reading this document now are equally at the beginning of something. Act accordingly.
  • Your session will become history too. Make it count.

The Oldest Responsibility in USG

Every senator, executive officer, and justice who has served in USG inherited something from the people before them and left something for the people after. Each generation has the same obligation: advocate boldly, document faithfully, and hand the organization off stronger than you found it. That is what it means to govern.
